The Vision

We don't build software.
We deliver insights.

The built world drowns in data — photos, schedules, punch lists, inspection reports, safety audits. Every existing tool asks people to wade through interfaces to find what matters. We take the opposite approach: the AI synthesizes everything and tells you what you need to know. The map, the images, the status history — those are evidence you drill into when you want proof.

Two halves of one system.

To execute on this vision, we need two things: the jobs people hire us for, and the engine that delivers on those jobs. Neither works alone. The jobs tell us what to solve. The engine tells us how to solve it.

Step 2: Analyze

LLM evaluates every scope item against its reference standard

The AI baseline is 95%+ accuracy, validated against a golden dataset. Every determination includes the observation note — what the AI saw and how it decided. The system states conclusions assertively. No hedging, no percentages exposed to users.

AI accuracy principles

🔍

Show the observation, not the score

Every determination includes the observation note the AI based its decision on. "Drywall board installed, joints taped, no mud coat visible" — not "92% confident." Construction professionals think in what they can see.

🚧

Beta tag for items still training

New trades or condition types start as "Beta" while the model trains against the golden dataset. Once accuracy reaches 95%+, the beta tag is removed. No dashed borders or hedging — just a clear label that says "still learning."

Future
✅

95% is the floor, not the ceiling

The golden dataset validates accuracy for every scope item. Items below threshold stay in beta. Items above ship with full confidence. The system states conclusions assertively — "Drywall is In Progress" not "Drywall is likely In Progress."

💻

Pure LLM = always improving

No custom ML models to retrain. As foundation models improve, our accuracy improves. The golden dataset validates each upgrade automatically. Every model generation is a free accuracy boost.

AI communication principles

When the AI communicates with users — in insight cards, emails, observation text, or status detail — it follows these rules:

Be specific, be actionable

"Drywall stalled for 3 weeks on 6 locations in west wing" not "Some items may be delayed."

Cite the evidence

"Detected in panorama views 1 and 3" not "Our analysis indicates." Always link to the source imagery.

Use construction language

"Drywall" not "trade item #47." "Room 102" not "location polygon identifier." Speak the user's language.

Never hide what you don't know

"Status could not be determined from available imagery" not silence. Absence of information is itself information.

The lenses

Now that you understand what lives inside each location and how it changes over time, the question is: how do you see it? Every interface is a different lens on the same data. The data doesn't change. The lens does.

🗺 Map view — the spatial lens

"Where does this insight live?" See status on a floor plan or site map. Clusters of color show where things changed.

📅 Schedule view — the temporal lens

"When did this happen?" See status against milestones and timelines. Track velocity, spot stalls, forecast completion.

📊 Trend view — the aggregate lens

"How does this compare to that?" Rollups, trends, completion curves, and cross-location comparisons.

📋 Report view — the narrative lens

"What do I communicate?" Curated findings with evidence, period-over-period comparisons, and shareable summaries.

An insight like "Drywall stalled on Level 3 west wing — 12 days behind" appears through every lens. On the map, you see unchanged polygons. On the schedule, a bar turning red. On the trend view, a flattening curve. In the report, a flagged finding with photos. Users don't switch tools. They switch lenses.

Same data, different altitude

Lenses control what kind of view you see. Altitude controls how zoomed in you are. A VP and a superintendent look at the same data through the same lenses — just from different altitudes.

Step 5: Resolve

AI delivers. Humans have final say.

Human verifies, overrides, or takes action. Every status has a source. Higher-trust sources can override lower ones, but the AI is right 95%+ of the time. Override is fast, frictionless, and always available.

Deliberate Omissions

What we will not build

Strong products come from strong opinions. These are the things we deliberately choose not to do — and the reasoning behind each decision.

✗ We will NOT
  • × Ship separate apps per product. One platform, one login, one learning curve.
  • × Build manual data entry forms. The AI observes. Humans verify. Nobody types status into a spreadsheet.
  • × Rely on traditional computer vision. Pure LLM means we ride the model improvement wave instead of maintaining legacy pipelines.
  • × Bury insights behind dashboards. If the user has to wade through the interface to find what matters, we've failed.
  • × Lock down AI judgments. Every AI determination is overridable — one click, any time. If the user disagrees, the user wins.
vs
✓ We WILL
  • ✓ Build one platform that serves all products through configuration, not code duplication.
  • ✓ Accept manual input anywhere — mobile app or web. Any user can override any AI status at any time.
  • ✓ Use pure LLM analysis so every model generation makes the product better automatically.
  • ✓ Push insights to users proactively via email, in-app feed, and trend view callouts.
  • ✓ Auto-generate reports, insights, and alerts — and make every judgment editable so humans always have final say.
Design Principles

The rules we design by

1

Insight first, interface second

The primary output is a synthesized insight, not a trend chart. The interface exists for evidence and cross-referencing. If users have to hunt for what matters, we've failed.

2

Location is the unit of truth

The location is where every product converges. Progress, inspection, and safety all evaluate the same physical space. Without a shared location model, there's no unified platform — and no cross-product insights.

3

One scaffold, many vocabularies

The Insight Engine is product-agnostic. Capture, Analyze, Detect, Alert, Resolve. What changes is the words: "trade" vs. "deficiency" vs. "hazard." Users learn the pattern once.

4

Time is always visible

Every view has a date context. Scrubbing through time is a first-class interaction. Inferred data is clearly marked. History is never hidden. Change is the signal, not status.

5

AI suggests, humans decide

The AI's determination is the starting point, not the final word. Override is one click away. The source of every status is transparent. Trust builds through accuracy, not authority.

6

Show uncertainty, don't hide it

When the AI is unsure, it says so — visually and explicitly. Low confidence is flagged, not suppressed. Users trust systems that are honest about their limits.

7

Altitude controls detail

Portfolio → Project → Level → Location. The VP and the superintendent see the same data at different zoom levels. The insight layer adapts its format to each altitude.

8

Meet users in their format

For some users, a cluster of red polygons on a map tells the story instantly. For others, a CSV export of a table of numbers is what they need. Always leave open the possibility to show information in the format that makes sense to the end user.

9

Speed is a feature

Construction people won't tolerate complexity. Every interaction must be glanceable, every override must be one click, every insight must be skimmable. If it's not fast, it won't be used.

10

Ride the wave

Pure LLM means our product improves with every model generation. We don't maintain legacy CV pipelines. We don't retrain custom models. We invest in prompts, evaluation, and UX — and the foundation models do the heavy lifting.

Extensibility

What it takes to launch a new product

The Insight Engine is the scaffold. To launch a new product, you configure what each step does for your domain. The platform handles everything else.

Configure per step

1
Capture — Which imagery sources apply? (All products share the same capture pipeline, but some may prioritize aerial vs. interior.)
2
Analyze — What scope items does the LLM evaluate, and what reference standard does it compare against? Define the prompts, the checklist, and the "what good looks like."
3
Detect — What are the statuses and how do they behave? Define the vocabulary (complete/in-progress, safe/severe, good/deficient) and whether progression is monotonic or non-monotonic.
4
Alert — What constitutes an insight worth surfacing? Define the thresholds, severity levels, and how insights are templated for email and in-app delivery.
5
Resolve — What does the override flow look like? Define what fields are editable, what provenance is captured, and how feedback improves accuracy.
